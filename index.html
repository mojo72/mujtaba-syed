The Real Me

20 Questions - Unfiltered

Beyond the resume: the mindset, values, and stories that shape how I think and work.

Q 01 What's the riskiest thing you have done in life?
Quitting a stable software engineering career at TCS to pursue an MBA and pivot entirely into product and customer success, with no safety net and no guarantee it would work. Mumbai doesn't reward ambiguity easily, and I had no roadmap. It was the best decision I ever made.
Q 02 Choose between money or power? Why?
Power, specifically the power to influence outcomes. Money follows impact, but power to shape a product, a team, or a customer's trajectory creates a multiplier effect. I'd rather be in the room where decisions are made than be compensated for the aftermath of someone else's decision.
Q 03 Are you usually more spontaneous or calculative? Why?
Calculative in strategy, spontaneous in execution. I map the terrain carefully: the customer's pain points, the team dynamics, the risks. But once I commit to a direction, I move fast and adapt on the fly. Pure spontaneity is chaos; pure calculation is paralysis. The edge lives between them.
Q 04 Do you usually strive for speed or perfection? Why?
Speed with a quality floor. Perfection is a myth in fast-moving SaaS environments; the market doesn't wait. I've shipped features that weren't perfect but solved real problems, and iterated from there. That said, I will never trade speed for trust. A rushed customer interaction costs more than a delayed one.
Q 05 What's more important: the institute name or the course you study? Why?
The course. I studied Mechanical Engineering, then did an MBA in Operations and Marketing. None of it was a conventional path to tech SaaS. What mattered was the thinking framework each discipline gave me. No brand name teaches you how to run a war-room during an IPL-scale outage. Real-world reps do.
Q 06 What's something you know you should do but keep procrastinating on?
Building my personal brand consistently: writing, posting, sharing frameworks publicly. The blocker is perfectionism disguised as humility. I tell myself my ideas aren't ready, but the truth is I'm afraid of being wrong in public. The irony is that every time I do share, it creates value. I'm working on shipping the thought, not the thesis.
Q 07 Why do you get angry? How often?
I get angry when avoidable mistakes repeat themselves, when systems fail people who trusted them, or when effort is clearly absent. It's rare, maybe a handful of times a year. I've learned that anger is useful as a signal but destructive as a tool, so I've trained myself to pause, diagnose, and channel it into a fix instead.
Q 08 Why do you lose patience? How often?
Slow decision-making in urgent situations. When a customer is bleeding and internal processes move at committee pace, I feel it acutely. It happens a few times a month. I've built the reflex of asking "what can I unblock right now?" rather than waiting for perfect alignment; that shift changed how I lead war-rooms entirely.
Q 09 When was the last time you were intimidated?
Walking into my first C-suite QBR at an Enterprise account early in my CS career. A room full of VPs and CTOs, and I was the one accountable for their platform outcomes. What killed the intimidation was focusing on the data: I knew their numbers better than they did. Preparation is the antidote to intimidation.
Q 10 When was the last time you had self-doubt?
Every time I step into a new scope. When I moved from Head of Onboardings to APM at CleverTap, I questioned whether I could think like a product person. The doubt lasted about three weeks before momentum took over. Self-doubt, for me, is just the body's way of saying "this is new territory, pay attention." I've learned to treat it as a good sign.
Q 11 What would your worst enemy say about you?
That I can be relentlessly direct to the point of bluntness, and that I move faster than people around me are comfortable with. Probably also that I'm stubborn when I believe I'm right. I think they'd be correct on all three. I'd rather be direct and wrong than ambiguous and safe. Stubborn conviction, paired with evidence, has served me well.
Q 12 List at least 3 things you have learnt about life.
1. Consistency beats intensity: showing up every day at 80% outperforms sporadic 100% sprints.
2. People remember how you made them feel, not what you delivered, especially in customer-facing roles.
3. The quality of your questions defines the quality of your outcomes. Ask better questions, get better answers, build better things.
Q 13 Share the latest new thing you have learnt.
Observability as a discipline, not just monitoring dashboards but understanding the full causal chain of system behavior. Working at Last9 fundamentally changed how I think about reliability. I can now read OpenTelemetry traces, correlate logs with latency spikes, and walk into an engineering conversation as a peer, not a translator.
Q 14 In 2-3 years from now, what do you think you will be celebrating?
A step into a VP or Chief Customer Officer role at a company building something genuinely transformative, and the first time a team I've built runs a QBR without needing me in the room. That's the benchmark for me. Not the title, but the evidence that I've multiplied myself through people.
Q 15 What would you do differently if you knew nobody would judge you?
I'd ship ideas in public much faster: frameworks, opinions, failures included. I hold back too much in the name of "getting it right first." I'd also take more creative risks in how I present to executives, less slide deck, more live storytelling. Judgment is the tax I pay for playing in public. I'm raising my risk appetite.
Q 16 Rate your self-worth on a scale of 1 to 10. Why?
8. I know what I'm capable of and I back myself in high-stakes situations. The two points I leave open are intentional; they're the discomfort I use to stay hungry. The moment I give myself a 10 is the moment I stop growing. Confidence without hunger is complacency, and I refuse that.
Q 17 What talent do you have that you are currently not using?
Two, actually. Both feel less like hobbies and more like roads I took a long look at and chose not to walk down yet.

The first is teaching. At a very young age, I was a mathematics teacher at one of Mumbai's stock-listed coaching institutes. There is a rare kind of satisfaction in taking a concept that intimidates most people and making it genuinely click for a student. I haven't been able to pursue it full-time or even part-time since, but when life gives me the space, that is the path I would walk toward without hesitation.

The second is photography. I have been a professional photographer with multiple personal assignments to my name: portraits, events, moments worth holding still. It never scaled the way it could have because other commitments always came first. But the eye for light, composition, and story never left. It is still there, waiting.
Q 18 Where do you draw the most energy from?
From the moment a customer goes from frustrated to unstoppable. There is nothing like watching an enterprise team that was ready to churn turn into advocates because we solved the right problem in the right way. That feedback loop, solving hard problems for real people, is where I feel most alive. It's why I've stayed in customer-facing roles.
Q 19 What time do you generally get up? What time do you sleep?
Up by 6:00 to 6:30 AM. Sleep by midnight. The morning window is sacred: no meetings, no Slack, just thinking, reading, and setting intent for the day. I've found that how I spend the first 90 minutes determines the quality of the next 14 hours. The late nights are when I do my deepest work, writing, or building. I'm wired for both ends of the day.
Q 20 What do you believe you are best at?
Translating ambiguity into momentum. Whether it's a struggling customer relationship, a chaotic product roadmap, or a team that's lost direction: I walk into the fog, find the signal, and build a path forward that people can follow. That's the thread connecting everything I've done: software developer, PM, Head of Onboardings, CS Director. I make complex things move.
